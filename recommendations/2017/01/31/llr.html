Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) in The Context of Recommendations

31 Jan 2017 on recommendations

Understanding versus Using.

People have been using this thing like a recipe without understanding what's going on. I myself am guilty of this. Now I took the time and tried to understand the whole reasoning behind this method. And, to my astonishment, I found some fundamental issues with it that severely limit its applicability. Luckily, there's a fix.

Looking at all recorded history, do the events $A$ and $B$ co-occur more often than chance would suggest?

If the events $A$ and $B$ occur independently, then \begin{align} p(a, b) = p(a) \, p(b) \end{align} where $a \in \{A, \neg A\}$, $b \in \{B, \neg B\}$.

Furthermore, \begin{align} p(a|B) = \frac{p(a, B)}{p(B)} = p(a) = \frac{p(a, \neg B)}{p(\neg B)} = p(a|\neg B) \end{align}

This means that the distribution of $A$ given the presence of $B$ is the same as the distribution of $A$ given the absence of $B$ ($\neg B$).

That will be our null hypothesis.

Model for a single trial

In the following, we encode $A$ as $1$ and $\neg A$ as $0$. We encode $B$ as $1$ and $\neg B$ as $0$.

Let's build a hierarchical model: \begin{align} p(a, b) = p(a|b) \, p(b), \end{align} where the model for $B$ will be a simple Bernoulli trial: \begin{align} p(b) = p_{B}^b \, p_{\neg B}^{1-b} \end{align} with $p_{B}$ ($p_{\neg B} = 1-p_{B}$) the latent probability of the occurrence (absence) event $B$.

The model for $A$ will be a Bernoulli trial where the probability of the event $A$ depends on the result of the $B$ trial: \begin{align} p(a|b) = p_{A}(b)^a \, p_{\neg A}(b)^{1-a} \end{align} with $p_{A}(b)$ ($p_{\neg A}(b) = 1 - p_{A}(b)$) the latent $b$-dependent probability of the occurrence (absence) event $A$.

Model for repeated trials

The statistics is completely determined by four counts: The number of trials, $n$, the total number of counts of event $A$, $k_{A}$, the total number of counts of event $B$, $k_{B}$, the number of times the events $A$ and $B$ occurred together, $k_{A \wedge B}$.

\begin{align} p(a_{1:n}, b_{1:n}, k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B) = \begin{cases} p(a_{1:n}, b_{1:n}), & \text{$k_{A \wedge B} = \sum_i a_i b_i$, $k_A = \sum_i a_i$, $k_B = \sum_i b_i$} \\ 0, & \text{else} \end{cases} \end{align}

Trials are independent: \begin{align} p(a_{1:n}, b_{1:n}) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(a_i, b_i) \end{align}

\begin{align} p(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B) & = \sum_{a_{1:n}, b_{1:n}} p(a_{1:n}, b_{1:n}, k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B) \end{align}

\begin{multline} p(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B) = \frac{n!}{k_{A \wedge B}! \, k_{A \wedge \neg B}! \, k_{\neg A \wedge B}! \, k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}!} \, p_A(B)^{k_{A \wedge B}} [1-p_A(B)]^{k_{\neg A \wedge B}} \\ \times p_A(\neg B)^{k_{A \wedge \neg B}} [1-p_A(\neg B)]^{k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}} p_B^{k_{B}} [1-p_B]^{k_{\neg B}} \end{multline}

\begin{align} k_{A \wedge \neg B} & = k_A - k_{A \wedge B} \\ k_{\neg A \wedge B} & = k_B - k_{A \wedge B} \\ k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B} & = n - k_A - k_B + k_{A \wedge B} \\ k_{\neg B} & = n - k_B \end{align}

Maximum Likelihood Ratio

$p(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B; p_A(B), p_A(\neg B), p_B)$

The maximum likelihood ratio: \begin{align} \lambda(A, B) & = \frac{\max_{p_A, p_B} p(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B; p_A, p_A, p_B)}{\max_{p_A(B), p_A(\neg B), p_B} p(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B; p_A(B), p_A(\neg B), p_B)} \end{align}

The maximum of the numerator is assumed for \begin{align} p_A & = \frac{k_A}{n}, & p_B & = \frac{k_B}{n}. \end{align} The maximum value is \begin{align} p\left(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B; \tfrac{k_A}{n}, \tfrac{k_A}{n}, \tfrac{k_B}{n}\right) & = \frac{n!}{k_{A \wedge B}! \, k_{A \wedge \neg B}! \, k_{\neg A \wedge B}! \, k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}!} \, \frac{k_A^{k_A} k_{\neg A}^{k_{\neg A}} k_B^{k_B} k_{\neg B}^{k_{\neg B}}}{n^{2n}} \end{align}

The maximum of the denominator is assumed for \begin{align} p_A(B) & = \frac{k_{A \wedge B}}{k_B}, & p_A(\neg B) & = \frac{k_{A \wedge \neg B}}{k_{\neg B}}, & p_B & = \frac{k_B}{n}. \end{align} The maximum value is \begin{align} p\left(k_{A \wedge B}, k_A, k_B; \tfrac{k_{A \wedge B}}{k_B}, \tfrac{k_{A \wedge \neg B}}{k_{\neg B}}, \tfrac{k_B}{n}\right) & = \frac{n!}{k_{A \wedge B}! \, k_{A \wedge \neg B}! \, k_{\neg A \wedge B}! \, k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}!} \, \frac{k_{A \wedge B}^{k_{A \wedge B}} k_{A \wedge \neg B}^{k_{A \wedge \neg B}} k_{\neg A \wedge B}^{k_{\neg A \wedge B}} k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}^{k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}}}{n^n} \end{align}

Log-Likelihood Ratio

The log-likelihood ratio: \begin{align} - 2 \log \lambda(A, B) = 2 n \, MI(A, B) \end{align}

$MI(A, B) = H(A) + H(B) - H(A, B)$ the mutual information.

\begin{align} H(A, B) & = - \frac{k_{A \wedge B}}{n} \log \frac{k_{A \wedge B}}{n} - \frac{k_{A \wedge \neg B}}{n} \log \frac{k_{A \wedge \neg B}}{n} - \frac{k_{\neg A \wedge B}}{n} \log \frac{k_{\neg A \wedge B}}{n} - \frac{k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}}{n} \log \frac{k_{\neg A \wedge \neg B}}{n} \\ H(A) & = - \frac{k_A}{n} \log \frac{k_A}{n} - \frac{k_{\neg A}}{n} \log \frac{k_{\neg A}}{n} \\ H(A) & = - \frac{k_B}{n} \log \frac{k_B}{n} - \frac{k_{\neg B}}{n} \log \frac{k_{\neg B}}{n} \end{align}

From https://github.com/tdunning/python-llr

llr_2x2(k11, k12, k21, k22) - Compares two binary conditions to see if there is an apparent connection. The idea is that we have made a number of observations that exhibit condition A or B. The number of times we have seen A and B is k11. The number of times we have seen A but not B is k12, not A but B is k21 and not A and not B is k22. Commonly, these are arranged in a so-called contingency table:

A not A B k11 k12 not B k21 k22 The idea is that llr_2x2 will give a large score if it appears that there is a correlation or anti-correlation between A and B. We don't get any idea whether A and B occurs more than we expect or not, however.

llr_root(k11, k12, k21, k22) - Computes a signed value much like llr_2x2 that has an added sign that tells us about whether the k11 cell is larger or smaller than we might expect if A and B are independent.

The actual value returned by llr_root is the square root of the value returned by llr_2x2. The reason for this is that in the absence of any correlation between the two conditions, the the output llr_2x2 will be chi-squared distributed. That distribution is the same as the distribution of the square of a normally distributed value. Since the chi-squared distribution cannot have a negative value, but the normal distribution can, it makes sense to take the square root at the same time as we add the sign.

Another nice benefit of taking the square root is that it allows us to interpret the output of llr_root as some number of standard deviations.
