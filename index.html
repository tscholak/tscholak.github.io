Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) in The Context of Recommendations

Looking at all recorded history, do the events $A$ and $B$ co-occur more often than chance would suggest?

If the events $A$ and $B$ occur independently, then \begin{align} p(a, b) = p(a) p(b) \end{align} where $a \in \{A, \not A\}$, $b \in \{B, \not B\}$.

Furthermore, \begin{align} p(a|B) = \frac{p(a, B)}{p(B)} = p(a) = \frac{p(a, \not B)}{p(\not B)} = p(a|\not B) \end{align}

Where To Start Reading?

I recommend starting with Russ Salakhutdinov's article about Bayesian probabilistic matrix factorization. Alex Beutel's article should be next. The issue of side information should be postponed since it adds further complications.

Most of imm's code is based on the approach described in Dilan Görür's article. The inference algorithms used by the imm package are introduced and explained in the five articles in the section "Bayesian Inference Algorithms". Perhaps most interesting is the last article in that section: Ge et al. describe a distributed approach to inference for Dirichlet process mixture models.

Unfold with unfoldr

unfoldr is the second program of my complex systems software suite that I open-source. (histogramr was the first one.) For those of you who study the spectral features of complex systems such as random networks I think unfoldr will prove quite useful. Given the eigenvalues of an ensemble of random matrices, unfoldr calculates the nearest-neighbor level spacings of the unfolded spectrum, either as a whole or for slices of it. You can specify how you want to cut the spectrum into slices — linearly, logarithmically —, and unfoldr will calculate the level spacings for each slice individually. With this you can study how the level spacing statistics change with energy and whether or not there is a phase transition in the spectrum.
